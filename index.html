What is a Stock Exchange?

Think of it as a giant, organized supermarket. But instead of groceries, it's a marketplace for buying and selling tiny pieces of ownership in public companies. These pieces are called stocks. The exchange is the central hub that makes this all happen smoothly and fairly.

Why Do They Exist?

Stock exchanges play a vital role for both companies looking to expand and individuals looking to invest. They create a powerful connection that fuels economic growth.

üè¢ For Companies

Companies use the stock exchange to raise large amounts of money (capital) for growth. By selling stocks in an Initial Public Offering (IPO), they get the funds needed to build new factories, develop new products, and hire more people. It's like getting a huge investment from the public to fuel their biggest ambitions.

Place an Order

You decide to buy or sell a stock. You can't do this directly, so you place an order through a licensed firm called a broker (e.g., Fidelity, Charles Schwab, Robinhood). You specify which stock and how many shares you want to trade.

The Referee: Keeping the Game Fair

To ensure the market is safe and trustworthy for everyone, the U.S. government created a watchdog agency: the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). Think of it as the police force of the stock market.
